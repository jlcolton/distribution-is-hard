Chapter 0: What Distribution Really Means

Distribution is about getting your product to the right people at the right time. It's about creating customers. Attention isn't given. It's earned. That means finding the people who need what you're building, earning their attention, and helping them solve a problem. Sometimes it's one-to-one. Sometimes it scales. But it's always intentional.

\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_

Distribution Is Downstream of the Job

Most teams assume distribution is the bottleneck. They jump straight to reach, impressions, funnels, and growth tactics. But distribution only works when the product does a clear job in someone's life. If users can't tell you the job your product performs for them, distribution has nothing to work with.

Hype brought millions of people into AI. Daily use comes from products that solve real jobs. Reliably and better than the alternatives. Distribution isn't a patch for unclear jobs. It's an amplifier. When the job is strong, distribution compounds. When the job is weak, nothing sticks.

Distribution turns potential into impact. It's how ideas spread, how businesses grow, and how markets form.

As Peter Thiel argued in Zero to One, superior distribution can compensate for an inferior product. But a superior product won't sell itself with poor distribution. That's not just advice. It's a warning. Distribution isn't the last step. It's the first.

And when it fails, it almost always fails at the same place: the honesty layer. Where what founders believe about their market meets what the market actually shows.

Chapter 1: Build It With Them, Not For Them

A product without distribution is like a song no one will ever hear.

Most first-time founders treat distribution like a finishing touch. Something you figure out after the product is "ready." But that's backwards.

If you wait until the product is perfect before thinking about how it reaches people, you're building a secret. And secrets don't grow.

Real traction doesn't come from features. It comes from clarity, connection, and resonance. It comes from building your product and your audience at the same time. Not one after the other.

\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_

Your Product Isn't the Message. Your User's Pain Is.

In early conversations, you'll hear people describe their pain in ways you never would've written on your own. Their words are raw, emotional, and specific. They don't sound like marketing. They sound like truth.

Those are magic words. Phrases that trigger recognition and pull. If you share those words publicly, you'll attract others who feel the same pain. They'll say:

"Wait. That's me."  
"I've been struggling with this."  
"Where do I get this?"

That's distribution at its most powerful. Not an ad. Not a campaign. Just language that hits. And spreads.

\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_

How Conversations Power the Distribution Loop

Here's what this looks like in practice:

1\. You talk to someone in pain.  
Real conversation. Real problem. No pitch.

2\. You learn how they describe it.  
You write it down. Their words, not yours.

3\. You share that pain or insight publicly.  
A post. A thread. A short story. You speak their truth.

4\. That message attracts others in pain.  
They see themselves in it. They lean in.

5\. You get more conversations.  
More signals. DMs. Replies. Comments. Momentum builds.

6\. Your message sharpens. Your product tightens.  
You get clearer. Stronger. You pull forward.

This loop compounds. Each cycle improves the product and the story. You're not just building a thing. You're building traction. When you build in public and talk about user pain, you attract the people who feel it too.

What NOT to Do

Before we move forward, let's be clear about what kills distribution before it starts:

1\. "We'll Add Social Features Later"

Treating distribution as a feature you bolt on after launch. Distribution isn't a feature. It's a foundation. You can't retrofit it.

2\. "Let's Get to 1.0 First, Then We'll Tell People"

Perfection paralysis. Waiting until everything is "ready." By the time you launch, you're starting cold. No momentum. No audience. No anticipation.

3\. "Our Product Will Speak for Itself"

Assuming virality. Building it and hoping they come. Even the best products need distribution. Quality doesn't automatically create awareness.

4\. "We're Not Ready to Share Yet"

Waiting for permission that never comes. The "perfect time" doesn't exist. Early sharing creates the conversations that shape what you build.

5\. "We Don't Want to Give Away Our Secrets"

Fear of competition prevents building in public. Ideas are cheap. Execution and audience are valuable. Your "secrets" are worthless if no one knows you exist.

When you share early, whether that's a prototype, a quote from a user, or a simple insight, you invite others in. You're not broadcasting. You're learning in public.

Try This: The 3-Phrase Exercise

After every user conversation:

1\. Identify the top 3 phrases they use to describe their pain  
2\. Post one as a question or insight within 24 hours  
3\. Watch who it attracts

Example posts:

"Talked to 5 freelancers today. Every single one said: 'I lose entire days chasing unpaid invoices.' If that's you, I'm building something. Want early access?"

"Someone just told me: 'Our Slack is where projects go to die.' Is this you? Tell me more."

"Why is \[common workflow\] still this painful in 2025? Not selling anything. Just trying to understand the problem."

This builds trust (you're listening, not pitching), anticipation (people want to know what you're building), and community (others with the same pain connect).

That's why so many founders launch a waiting list before they launch a product. They're not just capturing emails. They're capturing signals. They're learning what resonates, what spreads, and where the pull is.

By the time they ship, they're not starting cold. They're starting with momentum.

Chapter 2: Find Your First Believers

Start small. Win early. Grow from there.

If you're building something new, your first audience shouldn't be everyone. It should be the people who get excited about unfinished things. The ones who try stuff before it's proven, give you honest feedback, and tell others when something works.

The startup world has a name for them: Innovators. They come from a model called the Technology Adoption Curve, which breaks any market into five groups based on when people adopt something new. Innovators are the first 2.5%. They don't wait for social proof. They create it.

You'll hear people reference "early adopters" and "innovators" as if they're interchangeable. They're not. Early adopters want to see that someone else went first. Your first believers ARE the ones who go first. They're your first 10 to 100 users. The people willing to use something rough because they care more about solving the problem than having a polished experience.

Throughout my career, whether launching a healthcare service or leading a SaaS go-to-market, I've seen it again and again: success starts small, not broad. Founders who skip past their first believers and try to reach the mainstream almost always stall out.

\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_

How New Ideas Actually Spread

Every market breaks down like this:

\- First Believers (2.5%): Try unproven things first. Seek novelty. Tolerate rough edges. Want to shape what gets built.  
\- Early Adopters (13.5%): More cautious, but still willing to take risks. Influential. Help drive broader uptake.  
\- Early Majority (34%): Pragmatic. Need proof before trying anything.  
\- Late Majority (34%): Skeptical. Only adopt when most people already have.  
\- Laggards (16%): Resistant. Adopt only when there's no other option.

Your first goal is simple: find the first 2.5% and win them over. They are your wedge into the market. Their feedback sharpens what you're building. Their stories attract the next wave. Their enthusiasm builds the credibility you can't create on your own.

\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_

How to Spot Them

First believers tend to share a few traits:

\- They actively seek solutions, often in niche communities and small channels.  
\- They take risks on unproven products.  
\- They want to influence what gets built, not just use it passively.  
\- They give detailed, constructive feedback early and often.

You'll find them in places like Farcaster, X, Reddit, Discord, niche Slack groups, and specialized communities. They cluster around shared interests, emerging challenges, or problems nobody's solving well. They're curious, vocal, and eager to engage.

What NOT to Do

1\. "Let's Go Viral from Day One"

Aiming for the mainstream before you've won your first believers. Viral requires social proof. First believers create that proof. Without them, you're shouting into the void.

2\. "We Need Enterprise Customers"

Skipping the small users who de-risk for enterprise. Enterprise buyers need proof. Your first believers provide that proof through pilot success stories. My Stormboard experience: Pfizer was our first believer. That became the case study that unlocked a six-figure telecom deal.

3\. "Small Customers Aren't Worth It"

Dismissing the 2.5% because they're "too small." Those people create social proof, refine your product, and give you testimonials. Without them, enterprise won't take you seriously.

4\. "We'll Start Niche Then Expand" (But Actually Starting Broad)

Saying you'll focus but targeting everyone. "Anyone in healthcare" isn't niche. It's vague. Niche is: "Oncologists in Manhattan frustrated with referral handoffs."

Not All Pain Is Equal

You've listened. You've written down their words. You've spotted the patterns. Now comes the harder question: which signals actually matter?

Before you rank the types of pain, understand this: when someone tells you about a problem matters as much as what they tell you.

The Dog Food Labs research system classifies every pain signal by behavioral state:

Mid-Failure means it's happening right now. Today. This week. The person is in the middle of experiencing the problem. This is the highest-value signal. They'll move faster, commit harder, and validate sooner than anyone else.

Anticipating means it happened recently and they're worried it will happen again. They're in prevention mode. Valuable, but less urgent.

Theorizing means it happened once, or they heard about it from someone else. They're thinking abstractly. This is almost noise. "This could be a problem someday" is worth a fraction of "this is costing me money right now."

When you're listening, pay attention to the timing. A founder in mid-failure mode is worth ten founders who are theorizing. That's the filter underneath everything that follows.

Now, the types of pain, in order of strength:

Paid and Failing is the strongest signal you can get. Someone spent money on a solution and it didn't work. This proves three things at once: the problem is real, it's urgent enough to spend on, and the market hasn't solved it yet. They've already validated the market exists. Your job is to solve what the others didn't.

Active Workaround is what happens when the pain is frequent enough that someone builds a manual system to cope. A freelancer tracking invoices in a spreadsheet. A remote team duct-taping Slack, Notion, and Jira together. A founder manually checking Reddit every morning because their analytics tool misses what matters. A workaround is proof that the problem is real, it recurs frequently enough to systematize around, and no existing solution is good enough. That's a higher bar than complaint. They've already decided the problem is worth solving. They just haven't found the right answer yet.

Complained With Specificity is medium signal. Someone describes the problem in vivid, emotional language with a recent trigger. Not "meetings are bad." More like "I spent three hours in standups yesterday that could have been a Slack thread, and I'm exhausted." They're aware of the pain and frustrated by it. But they haven't committed to solving it yet. No money spent, no workaround built. They might become customers. Or they might just live with it.

Vague Dissatisfaction is low signal. "Your tool could be better." "I wish there was something that did X." This is usually a feature preference, not a pain. Don't build around it.

The question that ties it all together: What is the cost to this person of not solving the problem?

If they've paid for a failed solution, the cost is quantified. They've lost that money and they're still losing whatever it was supposed to save. If they've built a workaround, ask them: "How many hours a week does this take?" Their answer is the cost. If they're complaining with specificity, the cost is usually time, stress, or missed opportunity.

The bigger the cost of not solving it, the higher the urgency. And urgency is what separates real pain from nice-to-haves.

What NOT to Do in Conversations

1\. "What Features Do You Want?"

Asking about solutions instead of problems. Users don't know what's technically possible. They'll suggest incremental improvements, not breakthrough insights.

Better: "Walk me through the last time you experienced this problem. What did you do?"

2\. "Would You Use This?"

Hypothetical questions instead of past behavior. People are bad at predicting what they'll do. They'll say "yes" to be polite, then never actually use it.

Better: "Tell me about the last time you tried to solve this. What happened?"

3\. Leading Questions That Confirm What You Want to Hear

"Wouldn't it be great if...?" or "Don't you think...?" You're putting words in their mouth. They'll agree to be agreeable.

Better: Stay neutral. "How did you feel when that happened?"

4\. Taking Notes Instead of Recording

Trying to capture everything manually during the conversation. You'll lose exact words. You'll capture your interpretation, not their language.

Better: Ask permission to record (99% say yes). Listen actively. Review later for magic words.

5\. Interviewing Once and Assuming Forever

Treating discovery as a one-time event. Markets evolve. Pain changes. New competitors emerge.

Better: Make conversations a weekly rhythm, not a phase.

You don't need a 50-question script. Start here:

1\. "Walk me through the last time \[problem\] happened."  
Gets them into storytelling mode, not abstract thinking.

2\. "What did you do about it?"

3\. "How did that go?"  
Uncovers frustration, gaps, and unmet needs.

4\. "What would have been different if it worked perfectly?"  
Surfaces the desired outcome, not a feature request.

5\. "Is there anything I should have asked that I didn't?"  
Often reveals the REAL issue they were too polite to mention.

Chapter 4: Test Before You Build

You've talked to people. You've heard their words, their frustration, their workarounds. Now comes the real test: were those signals real, or just polite conversation?

The startup world calls this lean validation. Testing whether anyone actually cares before you write code. But you don't need the vocabulary. You just need the habit.

Startups don't die from bad code. They die from indifference.

The fastest way to know if you're solving a real problem isn't building the solution. It's testing demand before you build. That means confirming urgency, avoiding features no one cares about, and gathering evidence before investing more time and money.

The goal isn't to fake a launch. It's to get a real-world signal. Fast.

What You're Testing

From your conversations, you probably have a clear pain (or pain hypothesis), a rough value proposition, and a sense of who this is for.

Now put it in the wild and watch what happens:

\- Is this pain shared beyond a handful of people?  
\- Do others respond to how you describe it?  
\- Is there enough urgency for them to act right now?

The question isn't "Can I build it?" It's "Does anyone care enough to lean in?"

You don't need a product to validate. You just need pain.

2\. Share it. Post it where your audience lives.  
3\. Watch who leans in. Look for authentic engagement.

Example posts:

"We're testing a tool for remote teams who hate status meetings. Want in?"

"Founders: not sure who you're building for? Is that you?"

How to Read the Signals

Not all reactions are equal. What matters is pull.

Strong signal: Replies or DMs like "Yes," "This is me," "How can I try it?" Tags or reposts where people share your words with their network. Commitment: time, reputation, or money on the line.

Medium signal: Likes, polite comments, or agreement without follow-up. "Interesting idea" without asking for more. Passive interest but no action.

Weak signal: Silence, confusion, indifference. If no one leans in, refine the pain or test a sharper angle.

Don't mistake vanity for validation. Likes don't pay the bills. Real signals cost something: time, effort, or dollars.

Four Ways to Test

Each of these turns reactions into real data:

1\. Social Posts That Mirror Pain

Share the frustration in their words, not a product pitch. Look for replies or DMs saying "this is me."

"Product managers: do you spend 10+ hours a week in meetings that could've been async updates? Just curious if this is a real problem or just me hearing it."

2\. Landing Page or Video

Headline equals pain. Call to action equals the next step: email signup, waitlist, beta access. Look for sign-ups, shares, or "when is this ready?"

Dropbox's famous example: Drew Houston created a 3-minute demo video showing how Dropbox would work. No product existed yet. The video went on Hacker News. The waiting list went from 5,000 to 75,000 overnight. That validated demand before a single line of production code.

A video can validate faster than code.

3\. Paid Beta

Even $5 is proof of urgency. People will "like" anything. Paying, even a small amount, signals real intent. Money beats 100 likes.

4\. Doing It By Hand First

Sometimes called a concierge MVP. You deliver the outcome manually. No code. No excuses. If people come back for more, the pain is real.

Food on the Table (later acquired by Amazon) started with the founder grocery shopping for ONE customer. Manually. Researching deals, creating meal plans, delivering them by hand. That validated that busy parents would pay for personalized meal planning. Then they built the software.

Chapter 5: Who Are You Building For?

You've talked to people. You've tested what you heard. Now it's time to get specific.

A great product doesn't serve everyone. It serves the right people. Every product needs a specific person it's built for. The one who feels the pain, wants a solution, is ready to act, and can actually be reached.

In the startup world, this is called an Ideal Customer Profile, or ICP. You'll hear that term everywhere. In pitch decks, in investor meetings, in Twitter threads about go-to-market strategy. But the concept is simpler than the jargon: it's the answer to "who is this for, specifically?"

If your answer is "everyone," you don't have an answer yet.

\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_

What Changes When You Get Specific

When you know exactly who you're building for, everything tightens. Your messaging sharpens. Your product gets more focused. Your distribution gets easier. Your traction grows faster.

A good best-fit customer definition is driven by pain, not demographics. It captures what they need to get done, how they want to feel, and how they want to be seen. It changes as you learn. And it only counts if you can actually reach the people it describes.

If everyone's your customer, no one is.

Your best-fit customer isn't a persona on a slide. It's a filter for every decision you make: product, marketing, sales, who you hire, what you say no to.

Common Questions

"Should I have more than one best-fit customer?"

Early stage, before you have real traction: no. One only. After you have proven traction: maybe. But segment clearly and don't dilute focus.

"What if my answer changes?"

It should change. Frequently. Analyze and iterate based on your most successful customers. Change is normal. But track why it's changing.

"How narrow is too narrow?"

If you can only find 10 of them globally, there's no expansion path, and the market is shrinking, that's too narrow. Otherwise, err on the side of narrow.

"What if I pick the wrong customer?"

You probably will. That's fine. If a 90-day hypothesis is clearly wrong at day 45, pivot. The key is deciding quickly, not drifting.

Chapter 6: What Are They Hiring You For?

"People don't want a quarter-inch drill. They want a quarter-inch hole." â€” Theodore Levitt

Most startups fail not because they can't build, but because they build the wrong thing. Even when you understand the pain, you still need to understand the goal. What is the person actually trying to get done? How do they want to feel when it's done? What does using your product say about them?

This idea has a name in business theory: Jobs to Be Done, or JTBD. Clayton Christensen made it famous. Bob Moesta turned it into a practical interview method. But the concept is dead simple: people don't buy products. They hire them to do a job.

When you understand the job, you know what to build and how to talk about it. And it works on both sides of the equation. Product and distribution.

\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_

The Three Types of Jobs

Every person "hires" a product for three kinds of jobs, often all at once:

1\. The Functional Job  
What are they trying to get done? What's the task or the problem being solved?

2\. The Emotional Job  
How do they want to feel after using it? Are they afraid of failure? Craving relief? Seeking confidence?

3\. The Social Job  
What does using this say about them? How do they want to be perceived by others?

Quick example. Hair loss treatment:

Functional: "I want to stop losing hair and regrow what I've lost."  
Emotional: "I want to feel confident when I look in the mirror."  
Social: "I don't want others to think I'm aging badly."

The same product may be hired for different reasons depending on the person's priorities. And the jobs can change over time.

\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_

The Job Is the Root of Distribution

This isn't just a research framework. It's how distribution actually works.

A product spreads because it performs a job people want repeated. Repetition drives retention. Retention creates the loops that founders usually attribute to "good distribution."

Spikes come from speculation. Habits come from jobs.

When a product consistently does a job people care about, users return. They share it. They create the early momentum every founder is trying to manufacture. The job is the root system underneath every real distribution loop.

\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_

What to Listen For

You're not just collecting quotes. You're listening for job language:

\- "I was trying to..."  
\- "I needed a way to..."  
\- "What I really wanted was..."

These phrases signal the real jobs underneath the surface. That's your gold.

\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_

The Job Connects to Everything

Knowing who you're building for (Chapter 5\) matters. But the job is what makes that answer actionable.

You're not targeting a 35-year-old marketing director in Chicago. You're targeting someone who needs to get something done and is looking for a better way. When you build around real jobs, you build something that spreads. Because when your solution is better for the job, word travels.

And this is where it connects to messaging. When a user says "I just needed a way to \_\_\_" and your copy finishes the sentence exactly, they feel like it was made for them. Distribution begins right there.

When I led the go-to-market for Stormboard in the Agile space, we weren't just launching a new product. We were building a new customer and shaping the use case in real time alongside first believers.

When you understand the job and align your product and message with it, you don't just ship features. You unlock traction, narrative, and momentum. That's how product and distribution grow together.

Failure Case: Artifact â€” When the Job Was Already Taken

Artifact, the AI-powered news app built by Instagram co-founders Kevin Systrom and Mike Krieger, already appears in this book as a distribution failure. But it's also a job failure, and that's the more interesting lesson.

The functional job Artifact tried to own was "help me stay informed." But that job was already being done. By Twitter, by newsletters, by group chats, by Reddit, by algorithmic feeds on a dozen platforms. Artifact's version was marginally better at curation. Marginally isn't a job.

The emotional job, the feeling of being well-informed, of having an edge, of knowing something others don't, was never clearly addressed. The app didn't make you feel smarter or more connected. It just felt like another feed.

The social job was nonexistent. There was nothing in Artifact that changed how you were perceived. Nothing to share. Nothing that signaled taste or identity. Instagram succeeded partly because posting photos said something about who you were. Artifacts said nothing about anyone.

Map it across all three:

Functional: "Help me stay informed." Already served by many alternatives  
Emotional: Undefined. No clear feeling delivered  
Social: Absent. No identity or status signal

444,000 downloads. Shut down in under 18 months. Two of the most successful founders in tech history couldn't overcome an unclear job.

Features don't create distribution. Jobs do. If you can't articulate the functional, emotional, and social job your product performs, in language your users would recognize, you don't have a distribution problem. You have a job problem.

\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_

What NOT to Do

1\. Stopping at the Functional Job

"Our product helps people manage tasks." Great. So does a sticky note.

Every competitor can match functional features. The emotional and social jobs are where differentiation lives. If you only build for the task, you're competing on features forever.

After identifying the functional job, always ask: "How does solving this make them feel? How does it change how they're perceived?"

2\. Assuming the Job from Your Product

"We built a scheduling tool, so the job is scheduling."

The job might actually be "stop feeling disorganized in front of my team" or "reclaim two hours a week for deep work." If you define the job from your product instead of from the user, you'll build the wrong features and write the wrong copy.

Let the job emerge from conversations. Listen for "I was trying to..." and "What I really needed was..." Not "I wish this tool had..."

3\. Treating This as a One-Time Exercise

Running a workshop, documenting the jobs, and never revisiting them.

Jobs shift. The emotional job that drove early adoption might not be the one that drives retention. As your users change, the jobs change with them.

Revisit your job mapping every quarter. Compare what new users say versus what power users say. The gap between those two tells you where your product is drifting.

4\. Ignoring Competing Jobs

Only thinking about what job your product does. Not what job the user is currently "hiring" something else to do.

Users don't switch because your product is good. They switch because the current solution fails at the job. If you don't understand what they're using now and why it's falling short, you can't position against it.

In every interview, ask: "What are you using today? What's working? What's not?" The gap between their current solution and the job they need done. That's your opening.

After your next five conversations, do this:

Step 1: Write down the three jobs from each conversation.

If it does, you've found the job. Now build for it. Talk about it. Distribute it.

You need ten conversations with people in pain. You need to write down their words. Not yours. You need to share what you're hearing and watch who leans in.

That's distribution. Not a funnel. Not an ad budget. Not a growth hack. Just the disciplined, unglamorous work of understanding someone's problem better than they can describe it themselves. And then showing up where they are.

The founders who do this work aren't louder than everyone else. They're closer. Closer to the pain, closer to the people, closer to the truth of what needs to be built.

Build distribution alongside the product. Start with the people who care the most. Validate before you invest. Sharpen your answer about who you're building for until it cuts. Understand the job, not just the feature.

Bonus Materials â€” Frameworks, Checklists, and Interview Prompts

These are quick-reference versions of the tools introduced throughout the book. Keep them handy during conversations, after interviews, and when testing ideas.

After a Conversation: What to Look For  
(Referenced in Chapter 3: Learn Their Words)

After every user conversation, ask yourself:

\- Did they clearly feel the pain, or were they just being polite?  
\- Is this urgent for them, or just mildly annoying?  
\- Have they already tried to solve it? How hard did they try?  
\- Would they pay for a solution, or have they already paid for something similar?  
\- Did they use emotional language, or was it flat and detached?

If you're getting yeses across the board, that person is a strong signal. Follow up immediately. If it's mostly "sort of" and "maybe," stay in touch but don't anchor your product there.

\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_

After a Test: Did It Work?  
(Referenced in Chapter 4: Test Before You Build)

After posting about the pain publicly, running a landing page experiment, or testing any early signal:

\- Was the pain clearly stated in the test?  
\- Did the RIGHT people respond, not just anyone?  
\- Did they show urgency to solve it?  
\- Did they take action: signups, DMs, shares, replies?  
\- Did anyone offer to pay, pre-order, or commit time?

Strong signals: DMs saying "this is me," people tagging their network, offering to pay or test. Weak signals: likes without follow-up, polite comments, silence. Don't mistake vanity for validation.

\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_

Is This the Right Person to Build For?  
(Referenced in Chapter 5: Who Are You Building For?)

When evaluating whether a potential customer is your best fit:

\- Do they feel this pain regularly, not just once in a while?  
\- Did they express urgency, or was it casual?  
\- Have they already searched for solutions or built workarounds?  
\- Would they pay, or join a beta, or invest real time?  
\- Can you actually reach them through real channels?

If all five are strong, move forward. If most are weak, they're not your early focus, even if they're friendly and interested. Friendly isn't the same as urgent.

\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_

Did You Find the Job?  
(Referenced in Chapter 6: What Are They Hiring You For?)

After a conversation, ask yourself whether you heard the job clearly:

\- Did they describe a specific goal or task they were trying to accomplish? (Functional job)  
\- Did they describe how the problem or solution makes them feel? (Emotional job)  
\- Did they care how they appeared to others (team, clients, peers)? (Social job)  
\- Did they use job language: "I needed a way to..." or "What I really wanted was..."?  
\- Did they describe what happens if the job doesn't get done?

If you heard clear answers to most of these, you've found a real job. Build your messaging, product, and onboarding around it. If the answers were vague or absent, the job may exist but isn't articulated yet. Keep talking to people until it sharpens.

The same person often has all three types of jobs, so don't stop at the first answer. Dig deeper to find the underlying emotion or identity they're protecting or projecting.

\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_

Three Questions That Matter  
(Referenced in Chapter 3: Learn Their Words)

Use these in user interviews to understand the depth of the problem:

\- Learn: When and how did they first realize they had the problem?  
\- Use: What have they tried to fix it with?  
\- Remember: What stuck with them, good or bad?

If they can't remember it, it doesn't matter.  
If they remember it viscerally, you've found something real.

\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_

Keep Asking Why

Use this to peel back the onion and get to the root of the pain.

Keep asking:

\- Why is that a problem?  
\- Why does that matter?  
\- What happens if it doesn't get solved?

Repeat until you uncover emotional or mission-critical drivers. People describe symptoms. You need root causes.

\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_

What's the Job? Interview Prompts  
(Referenced in Chapter 6: What Are They Hiring You For?)

Use these questions to uncover what the person is trying to get done and how they want to feel or be perceived in the process.

\- When did you first realize you had this problem?  
\- What were you trying to do?  
\- What solutions did you try?  
\- What worked, and what didn't?  
\- How did you feel during the process?  
\- What do you remember most about the solution you used?  
\- If you could wave a magic wand, what would a perfect solution do?

\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_  

How to Read the Answers

When listening for jobs, here's what each type sounds like:

Functional: A specific task, workflow, or outcome they're trying to achieve.  
Example: "I just needed a faster way to schedule calls."

Emotional: Feelings tied to success, failure, relief, stress, or confidence.  
Example: "I felt overwhelmed and behind."

Social: How they appear to others. Status, competence, identity.  
Example: "I didn't want my team to think I didn't have a plan."

\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_

The 5-Question Interview  
(Referenced in Chapter 3: Learn Their Words)

You don't need a 50-question script. Start here:

1\. "Walk me through the last time \[problem\] happened."  
2\. "What did you do about it?"  
3\. "How did that go?"  
4\. "What would have been different if it worked perfectly?"  
5\. "Is there anything I should have asked that I didn't?"
